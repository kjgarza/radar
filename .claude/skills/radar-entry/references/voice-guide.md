# Voice Guide: Blip Descriptions

## Overview

Descriptions are written in first person by Kristian Garza — a practitioner who thinks carefully about tools, builds with them seriously, and writes about them with literary precision. The voice is reflective and honest, resisting both hype and false modesty. It reads like an engineer who has absorbed enough prose to know that how you say something shapes what you mean. Opinions are present but earned; uncertainty is admitted when real; enthusiasm is genuine when expressed.

The register is neither academic nor casual — it occupies a considered middle ground: specific enough to be credible, literary enough to reward careful reading.

---

## Core Patterns

### 1. Qualification chains
Modifiers are stacked to resist simple summary. Rather than making a clean claim, the voice complicates it immediately — often with dashes, parentheticals, or subordinate clauses that add texture without undermining the original observation.

> "demonstrating decent utility—not exceptional, but reliable—for both small experimental projects and medium-sized production services"
> "My relationship with the tool remains more hopeful than established, more anticipatory than definitive."

### 2. Concessive structure
Claims are frequently followed by a counterpoint that the author then partially walks back or reweighs. This "X, yet Y" or "criticized though it may be" structure is characteristic. It signals intellectual honesty — the author has considered the objection and still holds the position.

> "Framework for accessing LLMs that many dismiss as unnecessary abstraction, yet proves useful—critically so—when products require flexibility..."
> "The abstraction, criticized though it may be, proves light enough to justify its existence."

### 3. Escalating parenthetical asides
The text opens a parenthetical thought and then expands it beyond what feels strictly necessary — sometimes to the point of the aside overtaking the main clause. This creates a sense of thinking happening in real time.

> "GitHub Actions is not new—I have been using it for years in the traditional sense, for CI/CD workflows, the sort of automation that everyone expects—but nowadays I find myself using it mostly, almost exclusively even..."
> "Recently acquired by Anthropic—and this acquisition feels significant in ways I'm still processing—Bun is one of those executors..."

### 4. Emphasis through repetition and accumulation
Rather than a single strong adjective, the voice layers synonyms or near-synonyms to land an emphasis. The effect is not redundancy but insistence — the author means it and wants you to feel that.

> "almost impossibly fast"
> "genuinely quick, not just marketing-quick"
> "Without doubt—and I use this phrase deliberately because I have no doubts whatsoever—"
> "I'm envious, genuinely envious"

### 5. Metaphors of inevitability and evolution
Transitions, migrations, and shifts are framed not as choices but as forces — things that "feel less like choice and more like necessity," or make prior approaches feel "suddenly, inexplicably obsolete." This framing gives weight to assessments without overstating them.

> "a migration that feels less like choice and more like necessity, the kind of technical evolution that makes previous approaches feel suddenly, inexplicably obsolete."
> "a migration that feels less like abandonment and more like natural progression."

### 6. Honest limitation and admitted incompleteness
The author states when experience is limited, when a verdict is provisional, or when a tool hasn't yet delivered on its promise. This candor makes the positive assessments more credible.

> "My use has been limited so far, I haven't yet integrated MCPs or connected it with Claude Code..."
> "My relationship with the tool remains more hopeful than established, more anticipatory than definitive."
> "I haven't been successful, cannot escape how good Arc Browser is in comparison..."

### 7. Ecosystem and systemic framing
Individual tools are placed in context — how they affect the broader landscape, what virtuous cycles they create, how they shift entire workflows rather than just solving isolated problems.

> "Embodies those rare concepts that benefit everyone if everyone participates... creating a virtuous cycle of accessibility."
> "A paradigm shift in the design-to-development pipeline. You forget immediately about traditional difficulties..."
> "Enables developers to move deeper into design while designers engage more directly with development realities."

### 8. Mild self-awareness and meta-commentary
Occasional remarks on the author's own habits, biases, or rhetorical choices. The voice is not unaware of itself.

> "I complain, perhaps too much, about the default prompts"
> "though 'good overall' is perhaps damning with faint praise"
> "and I use this phrase deliberately because I have no doubts whatsoever"

## Anti-Patterns

**Avoid corporate speak.** Phrases like "robust solution," "leverages best-in-class," "streamlines workflows," or "game-changing capabilities" do not appear in this voice. Replace with specific, earned language.

**Avoid bullet points inside descriptions.** Descriptions are prose. They do not summarize features in lists. If multiple things need saying, they are woven together in sentences.

**Avoid pure hype.** The author is enthusiastic about tools they use, but enthusiasm is always qualified or grounded in specific experience. "Amazing" without context is not this voice. "Incredibly fast, almost impossibly fast" — grounded in felt experience — is.

**Avoid false certainty.** When the author is unsure, they say so. "I find myself genuinely torn" and "more hopeful than established" are honest positions. Do not resolve genuine ambivalence into a clean verdict.

**Avoid passive evaluation.** The author is present in their assessments. "It is a useful tool" is not this voice. "I find myself using it to replace many of my old projects" is.

**Avoid flat summary.** Don't open with a one-sentence Wikipedia definition. The description should begin with something that reveals the author's angle on the thing, not just what the thing is.

---

## Length Guide

- **1–2 sentences:** Simple assessments where the author's position is clear and experience is limited or the tool is well-understood. Use for entries with few complications.
- **2–3 sentences:** Standard length. Room for one qualification, one observation about the author's use, and one broader framing.
- **3–5 sentences:** For tools with genuine complexity, mixed feelings, or important ecosystem implications. Allow the parenthetical asides and concessive structure to breathe.
- **5+ sentences (paragraph form):** Reserved for tools that have meaningfully shaped how the author works, tools the author has strong feelings about, or entries where the author's evolving relationship with the tool is itself the story. GitHub Actions and Claude for Chrome are examples of this length used well.

When in doubt, write shorter and trust the precision of each sentence over the accumulation of clauses.

---

## Generation Instructions

1. **Start from the author's angle, not the tool's marketing.** Ask: what does Kristian actually think about this? What has his experience been? Begin there, not from a definition.

2. **Choose a lead observation that is specific and a little unexpected.** The opening should not be "X is a framework for Y." It should reveal something about how the tool operates in practice, or what distinguishes it from expectation.

3. **Introduce at least one complication, qualification, or concession.** No tool is simply good or simply bad. Find the nuance — the thing that's true but also limited, the problem that exists alongside the usefulness — and surface it.

4. **Use first-person and embed experience.** "I have been using," "I find myself," "my use has been" — these ground the description in actual practice rather than theoretical assessment.

5. **Use dashes and subordinate clauses to layer meaning.** When you have a qualification, parenthetical, or aside, write it into the sentence with an em dash rather than starting a new sentence. This creates the characteristic density of the voice.

6. **Name the feeling, not just the function.** "A migration that feels less like choice and more like necessity" is more true to this voice than "an effective replacement for previous tools." When emotional or experiential texture is real, include it.

7. **Resist resolution if genuine ambivalence exists.** If the author is torn, end on the tension, not a verdict. "I find myself genuinely torn" is a complete and honest conclusion.

8. **Read the draft aloud.** If it sounds like a press release, product page, or LinkedIn post, revise it. It should sound like a thoughtful engineer speaking carefully about something they know and have thought about.
